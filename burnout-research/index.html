New Research Reveals Why Successful Men Feel Empty

The science behind the achievement trap—and why identity shifting works when everything else fails

Dr. Philip Brickman, a psychologist at Northwestern University, won the lottery in 1978.

Not literally. But he studied people who did.

And what he found destroyed everything we think we know about achievement and happiness.

The Lottery Winner Study

Brickman wanted to know: Do people who win the lottery become happier?

Seems obvious, right? Of course they do.

They interviewed 22 lottery winners—people who'd won between $50,000 and $1 million (equivalent to $200K-$4M today).

Then they interviewed a control group of regular people. Then they asked both groups to rate their happiness on a scale of 1-5.

The Results

Lottery winners: 3.33 out of 5
Regular people: 3.82 out of 5

People who won the lottery were less happy than people who didn't.

Within 18 months, lottery winners returned to their baseline happiness level. As if nothing had happened.

Brickman, P., Coates, D., & Janoff-Bulman, R. (1978). Lottery winners and accident victims: Is happiness relative? Journal of Personality and Social Psychology.

They called this phenomenon: hedonic adaptation.

Your brain adapts to any positive change and returns to its set point.

The Achievement Trap

You know this feeling.

You worked for years to get the promotion. You finally got it. You felt amazing for... three weeks.

Then it became normal. And you started looking at the next level up.

This isn't a character flaw. It's neuroscience.

Your brain is wired to habituate to positive changes. It's an evolutionary survival mechanism—if our ancestors got too comfortable, they'd stop hunting, stop protecting themselves, stop surviving.

So your brain makes sure you never stay satisfied for long.

This is why: The six-figure salary stops feeling like "enough." The promotion you fought for feels empty. The house, the car, the title—none of it fills the void.

Because achievement doesn't change your identity. It just raises the bar.

The Identity-Behavior Loop

In 2012, researchers at Stanford studied why diets fail.

They took 400 people trying to lose weight and split them into two groups:

Group A: Set a goal to "eat healthier"
Group B: Were told to think of themselves as "healthy eaters"

Same diet plan. Same calorie targets. Same exercise routine.

After 6 Months

Group A: Lost an average of 4 pounds. 83% had quit the diet.

Group B: Lost an average of 19 pounds. 71% were still following the plan.

What was the difference? Identity.

Gardner, B., Lally, P., & Wardle, J. (2012). Making health habitual: the psychology of 'habit-formation' and general practice. British Journal of General Practice.

Group A tried to change their behavior. Group B changed who they believed they were.

You don't do what you think. You do what you believe you are.

Why High Achievers Feel Empty

Let's connect the dots.

Hedonic adaptation means achievement doesn't create lasting fulfillment.

Identity-behavior loop means lasting change only happens when you shift who you believe you are.

So here's the trap:

Most high-achievers build their entire identity around achievement.

  • I'm successful because I work 60 hours a week
  • I'm valuable because I make six figures
  • I'm respected because I have an impressive title

This creates two problems:

Problem 1: The achievement never feels like enough (hedonic adaptation)

Problem 2: You can't stop achieving without losing your identity

So you're stuck in a cycle: Achieve → Adapt → Feel empty → Achieve more → Adapt → Feel empty → Achieve more... Forever.

The Harvard Adult Development Study

The longest-running study on happiness started in 1938. They tracked 724 people for 85 years.

The question: What makes people happy?

The answer wasn't money. Wasn't success. Wasn't achievement. It was relationships.

The Part Nobody Talks About

The men who reported the highest life satisfaction weren't the ones with the most relationships.

They were the ones who felt most aligned with themselves.

They weren't pretending. They weren't performing. They weren't sacrificing who they were for what they thought they should be.

They were just... themselves.

Waldinger, R. J., & Schulz, M. S. (2010). What's love got to do with it? Social functioning, perceived health, and daily happiness in married octogenarians. Psychology and Aging.

The takeaway: You can have the relationship, the job, the income, the status—and still feel empty—if you're living as someone you're not.

Most people can't answer that question.

Because they've spent their entire adult life building an identity around achievement. And they're terrified that without it, they're nobody.
